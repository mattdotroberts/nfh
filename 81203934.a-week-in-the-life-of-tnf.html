You may recall a loose promise, on signing up for this newsletter, that you’d get an email every Tuesday. And, as you may have come to discover, this deadline is somewhat flexible. To paraphrase Jeff Goldblum’s chaos theory-loving scientist in Jurassic Park: “Life finds a way … of getting in the way.”

I’m going to go out on a limb here, but I think there are a few of you interested not just in what I write here, but the other Ws that go into the making of the newsletter: the when, where, and why, as well as the how (the honorary extra W.) This essay aims to pull back the curtain, and also share some of the sources, tools and secrets that keep TNF ticking on, week after week.

The When

I’m writing the first draft of this essay on a Monday morning, at my Wirecutter-approved standing desk, a stereotypically British cup of tea in hand, as I fix myself into “newsletter mode” for another weekly cycle. Here’s how a normal week looks:

Monday: Get started, slowly. I’ll spend 30 minutes on Monday morning doing a weekly review (I’ve found Tiago Forte’s framework a light touch way to keep on top of things) then I’ll start to whittle down the key tasks for the next few days. On any given week it’ll be a combination of TNF bits, consulting work, small business admin (accounting, invoices, all that fun stuff) and family tasks. I’ll aim to eat one big frog every day, and mark off a smattering of smaller supporting tasks. Most Mondays I’ll have a good idea of the “hero essay” for this week’s newsletter, and if I’m working on an illustration with Tony he’ll have had visibility on it for at least a week (a huge difference from when we were working together last year, and I didn’t really know what would be in Tuesday’s newsletter until the previous Friday night.) If I hit a wall with a piece, and can’t seem to crack it, I’ll take a look at something else in progress, seeing if that can be developed in time, giving the other essay a week or two to breathe. I’ll also be getting stuck into whatever work deliverables are required for this week.

Tuesday: This tends to be newsletter day. I’ll carve out a 4-6 hour window which kicks off as soon as I get back from the school run and pilates (my weekly non-negotiable exercise slot.) I’ll aim to send the newsletter out by 1.30pm CEST. This isn’t always possible, but a deadline gives me a much-needed kick up the arse. If I don’t get finished by Tuesday—when going long on a particular subject like choosing a place to live, or when real life and work deadlines get in the way, it’ll play on my mind, non-stop, until it’s out the door. The secret sauce of TNF is all in the edit, and the constant rewriting up to—and often after—I hit the send button. Every essay gets at least a dozen passes, with some of the bigger ones winding up with over 20. I’ll keep going, ruthlessly trimming all the fat, wanting the reading experience to feel akin to scissors gliding through wrapping paper. The odd strike of inspiration comes on the very last pass, a final flourish that can elevate a good essay into a great one.

Wednesday / Thursday / Friday: Once the weekly issue is done, I can take my foot off the newsletter gas. I’ll focus on other stuff: my “real” job that keeps the lights on; the family admin of school pickups, drop-offs, dinners and homework; trying to exercise or get out and walk somewhere: up in the mountains, through the city, along the beach. I’ll check in with my nieces and see how things are going over on TNF Instagram, share something on Twitter I’m researching for a future essay, and regularly checking into TNF community in Geneva. Friday brings a discussion thread on Substack and I’ll pop in and out throughout the day.

Saturday / Sunday: Always spent away from the laptop (unless the writing muse randomly strikes) and as offline as possible.

In and amongst the week there will be an evening or two where I’ll go deep on an essay. It’ll mainly happen when my wife is out seeing friends, and I’ll (hopefully) have gotten the kids to bed at a reasonable time, with a little energy left in the tank. There’s every chance I’ll slump onto the sofa and tune into one of the “me” shows (rather than the “we” shows … we all do this, right?) But on the occasions where evening inspiration strikes, I’ll put a record on, bring my laptop into the living room, and perch on top of my 3x8 Kallax, writing until I run out of steam, which often brings me to the other side of midnight

There’s also the ongoing discipline of regular reading, throughout the week, that supports the newsletter. As many writers will tell you, reading is one of the best ways to improve your own writing. I’m always juggling a few books: some physical, stacked in piles across the house; others on my Kindle, powered by my old SF library card and Libby (which I adore); logging them all in Goodreads, liberally highlighting and feeding it into Readwise, so it has the best chance of resurfacing when I need it. The Substack app is one of my most thumbed—I’ll spend time working my way through the many newsletters I subscribe to, collecting shiny things like a digital magpie, preparing the nest for the following Tuesday.

The How

Notion is the beating heart of The New Fatherhood. I’d be lost without it. My Notion contains everything—work-in-progress essays, new ideas, interview requests, guest essays, lessons learned, research papers (sourced with the help of Sci-Hub) and a whole load more. Everything starts in Notion, and that’s where it stays, until the point it gets copied into Substack and you sent to you. Notion is always there—on my Macbook, on my iPad, on my iPhone. It’s never far from hand.

I’ve already written about the discipline of noticing, and how “Several Short Sentences About Writing” has most influenced the way I write. It’s filled with wonderful nuggets:

“Most people have been taught what they notice doesn’t matter, 
So they never learn how to notice, 
Not even what interests them.

Noticing is about letting yourself out into the world, 
Rather than siphoning the world into you. 
It’s the catching your sleeve on the thorn of the thing you notice 
And paying attention as you free yourself.”

I’ve got a kanban board in Notion called “Saplings”. It’s my home for noticing. This is where The New Fatherhood gestates—there are currently 185 essays, in various forms of readiness, getting noodled on in a very non-linear process. Some of these are a couple of bullet points, a quote, an observation on an encounter with my kids—any kind of “sleeve on the thorn” moment. I might come back weeks later, or end up combining a few of them together into a coherent narrative—one very recent example was In Praise of the Middle, where the first draft was done in August 2021, and it lay dormant until October this year. Essays can sit there, almost finished, for months, waiting for a final piece of the puzzle to present itself, one moment of magic to elevate it to something that’s ready to share.

Having this backlog is always helpful. They say never start from a blank page, so I’ve always got something that I can be working on.

The Why

Why do I write this? Why do I spend 10-20 hours a week pulling this newsletter together? It’s a question I’ve been asking myself for two years now. I touched on it in the Ask Me Anything Q&A last weekend:

On "Futura Free," the closing track to Frank Ocean's Blonde (a close runner up to "In Rainbows as my favourite album this side of the year 2000) he talks about the healing power of his own music:

"Play these songs, it's therapy, momma. They payin' me, momma. I should be payin' them"

“Blonde” is never short of life advice. It’s one of my favourite albums to write to, and I think about that line often while I write. I think this newsletter doubles as a place for me to work my own shit out, with a weekly schedule that puts a forcing function on it. The oft-quoted line from Joan Didion—”I write so I know what I think”—also rings true here. I'm often thinking out loud, working out my own beliefs and point of view on the world, understanding what it means to be a parent today, to raise little humans whilst trying to show up for friends and family, and sharing my progress on the way.

Thinking out loud—a few stray thoughts and observations

How much time should I dedicate to this?

This newsletter isn’t my full-time job. It can’t be, unless it hits ~1000 paid subscribers, and, even then? I’m not sure that’s what I want. I know myself well enough to know that if this becomes a full-time gig—what I have to do rather than what I enjoy doing— I’ll begin to feel differently about it. I don’t want the spectre of necessity to overshadow the real reasons I started TNF: to work through my own baggage, and hopefully help other dads on their own journeys. That thought also influences my thinking on how to structure this endeavour—if I’m going to carry on with a “real job” that provides an income, should I rethink what to do with the money that comes in from subscribers? Could TNF be a non-profit? A B Corp? A triple-bottom line business? The Therapy Fund this month was the first experiment into the idea of reinvesting the money it makes into helping more dads, and it’s resonated well with subscribers, old and new (some of whom might be reading this right now.)

Daily progress versus playing the long game

“Only one thing on your to-do list every day?” I can imagine you wondering. But I’ve learned not to measure myself on the daily yardstick of progress. Recent books like Oliver Burkeman’s “Four Thousand Weeks” (which I wrote about here) have made us more comfortable casting off this obsession with productivity, and I was relieved to read Mason Currey’s “Daily Rituals” (he also writes a great newsletter) and learn that many great thinkers and writers of our age had long stints of non-productivity in between publishing their greatest works. We can be very hard on ourselves when looking at output on a day-to-day timeframe, but over a longer timeline the perspective changes any output—I’ve now published 84 weekly issues of the newsletter, with around 1,500 words a time, only counting the main essays. That’s well over 100,000 words, and edging towards 150,000. Playing the long game also makes me feel less guilty about …

Discipline (or lack of it)

Barcelona is one of the best places in the world to live. It’s magnificent. I love it here. October is fantastic, the sun has been putting in the hours all week, and I can feel outside constantly calling me: step away from the desk, take the dog out, end up on a plaza with a flat white. It’s a great city for serendipity, where you’ll often bump into a friend and spend the afternoon chatting on a terrace over some tapas. It’s a quality of life we intentionally created when leaving San Francisco in 2018, burnt out from overwhelming jobs and an insane cost of living, giving everything we had for someone else. Life here feels very different. But I sometimes wonder how much bigger and better TNF might be if I was living in a grey city, happy to chain myself to Notion every day. Do I want that? Of course not. But that doesn’t stop that niggling voice suggesting I should be spending more time at a laptop.

Am I the bad art friend?

One final thing, before I head into the night. Because I write about very personal topics, with my own life playing a huge part in my output, I need to stay mindful of how conversations with friends turn into inspiration for essays. It’s impossible to completely avoid this, so I always try to let people know when it’s going to happen. But I had a few run-ins last year when people felt misrepresented—like a friend who shared something with me, a comment that drove an incredible amount of reflection and personal growth, but felt he came off as cruel in the essay. There was something in this New York Times essay on The Bad Art Friend that really resonated, coming around the same time. Do I need to put structures in place to stop this from happening? Should I just start “changing names to protect the innocent”? And what happens when Padme and Bodhi get older, and want to make their way through these essays?

Answers, as usual, on a postcard please.
